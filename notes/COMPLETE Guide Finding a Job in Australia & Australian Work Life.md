## Chapter 1 — Things to Know Before Applying

- Work rights are the first factor: valid visa = dramatically easier job search
    
- From outside Australia: networking with recruiters and employers is critical
    
- Key self-assessment questions before applying:
    
    - What are your work rights (visa conditions)?
        
    - Have you tried applying yet? What were the results?
        
    - Are you familiar with the Australian resume and cover letter format?
        
    - Are you comfortable performing in interviews?
        
    - Are you realistic about your skill level and the roles you are targeting?
        
    - Where do you want to live — are there available jobs in that state?
        
- Skill-level realism: a recent graduate targeting a managerial role is not realistic
    
- If already on a valid working visa, physically moving to Australia is strongly recommended — employers strongly prefer locally-based candidates (Australian address increases response rate significantly)
    
- Create an action plan prioritizing what to do first to maximize chances

## Chapter 2 — Australian Workplace Culture

- Workplaces are generally **informal** with **flat/horizontal organizational structures**
    
- All employees are considered equal; everyone's opinion is heard
    
- Some companies are more hierarchical — depends on the organization
    
- **Typical hours:** 9 AM to 5 PM (office jobs), approx. 38 hours/week; flexibility is common
    
- Australians are relaxed, friendly, informal, and have a strong sense of humor
    
- Everyone is addressed by **first name or nickname** (e.g., Susan → "Suz")
    
- Core values: politeness, respect, honesty, directness
    
    - Common expressions: "thank you," "please," "sorry," "no worries," "it's my pleasure"
        
    - If you did not understand something, ask directly — this is seen as engagement, not weakness
        
- Workplaces are **highly multicultural** — colleagues from all over the world
    
    - Speaking a language other than English with a colleague in front of others who cannot understand = unprofessional
        
- **Eye contact** when speaking is expected (both genders)
    
- Greeting: handshake + "Nice to meet you" — **no cheek kissing**
    
- Punctuality: arriving late to work or meetings is considered rude; always notify in advance
    

## Behaviors to Avoid

- Saying "what" instead of "sorry/pardon?" — use "Sorry, can you say that again please?"
    
- Swearing, racist/offensive jokes, comments about appearance, gender, ethnicity → anti-discrimination laws are strong; fireable offenses
    
- Discussing politics or religion in the workplace
    
- Using "mate" in professional settings (context-dependent, e.g., construction is fine)
    
- Speaking another language in front of English-only colleagues
    

## Multicultural Perks

- Exposure to different foods, cultural celebrations (Chinese New Year, Diwali, Ramadan, etc.)
    
- Work drinks (Thursdays/Fridays at the pub) and team lunches are common social rituals
    
- Takeaway coffee culture is huge, especially in Melbourne
    
- End-of-year office parties in December are standard

## Chapter 3 — Finding a Job in Australia

## Step 1 — Know Your Work Rights

- Visa determines: full-time vs. part-time eligibility, single vs. multiple employers, duration
    
- International students: usually part-time only
    
- Permanent residents: full work rights
    

## Step 2 — Understand Contract Types

|Type|Description|
|---|---|
|Casual|Variable hours based on employer needs; flexible; no guaranteed hours|
|Fixed-term|Employment for a set period (3, 6, or 12 months); may be extended|
|Permanent|Ongoing employment; ends only by resignation or termination|

## Step 3 — Understand Superannuation

- A % of your salary is compulsorily directed into a **super fund** (retirement savings)
    
- Super fund = company that manages/invests the money until you retire
    
- Use the **ATO Super Comparison Tool** to compare funds
    
- Temporary residents/students/backpackers: can withdraw super before leaving Australia (specific eligibility requirements apply — see [ato.gov.au](https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/super-for-individuals-and-families/super/temporary-residents-and-superannuation/departing-australia-superannuation-payment-dasp))
    

## Step 4 — Research Your Industry

- Know required years of experience, in-demand skills, expected salary, required certifications
    
- Use recruiters for professional jobs — they are a valuable resource
    

## Step 5 — Use Job Search Platforms

- **Major sites:** Seek, Indeed, LinkedIn, CareerOne, EthicalJobs
    
- **Informal work:** Gumtree, Facebook Groups (beware of scams)
    
- **Students:** University job portals — internships, graduate roles, career counselors
    

> [!tip] Volunteer to Break In  
> If you have no local experience, volunteer first. Use **GoVolunteer**, **Seek Volunteer**, or **Volunteer in Australia** to find professional-environment volunteering (e.g., marketing, administration for nonprofits). Benefits: local references, understanding Australian workplace culture, English practice, community contribution.

## Step 6 — Understand Job Descriptions

- Job ads typically contain: company overview, role responsibilities, **key selection criteria (KSC)**
    
- **Key Selection Criteria** = essential skills required for the role
    
- You must demonstrate you meet the KSC through your resume, cover letter, and interview
    

## Step 7 — Prepare Resume and Cover Letter

- **Resume** = contact details, work rights, skills, employment history, education, achievements, interests
    
- **Cover letter** = who you are, why you want this role/company, why you are the right candidate
    
- Do NOT include: photo, age, gender, marital status, nationality (not Australian practice)
    
- **Customize** resume and cover letter for every single application
    

## Step 8 — Prepare for Interviews

- Keep a list of all companies applied to; save the job ads
    
- When a recruiter calls, you must remember the company and role immediately — shows motivation
    
- Prepare using the position description's responsibilities and KSC
    

## Step 9 — Paperwork

- **TFN (Tax File Number):** Apply immediately on arrival via ATO — highly confidential
    
- **Australian bank account:** Required for salary payment
    
- **Working with Children Check:** Required for any role working with under-18s
    
- **Police check:** Required if lived outside Australia in the last 10 years (some employers)
    
- **Driver's license:** Some roles require it
    
- Have all **qualifications ready and translated**

## Chapter 4 — Working in Australia (Hours, Salaries, Benefits, Probation)

## Work Hours

- Full-time: 38 hours/week, typically 9 AM – 5 PM
    
- Part-time: less than 38 hours
    
- Lunch break: 30 min to 1 hour (contract-dependent)
    
- Staying late is not valued in itself — results and performance matter
    

## Flexibility and Remote Work

- Flexible hours increasingly common (negotiate start/finish times)
    
- Hybrid work (some days office, some days home) now standard post-pandemic
    

## Leave Entitlements

|Leave Type|Entitlement|
|---|---|
|Annual leave|4 weeks per year (paid)|
|Sick leave|Up to 10 days per year (paid)|
|Parental leave|Up to 18 weeks paid (after 12 months with employer)|
|Other types|See [fairwork.gov.au](https://www.fairwork.gov.au/)|

- Work-life balance and mental health are genuinely valued
    
- Many companies shut down 2 weeks in December for the holiday period
    
- Giving adequate notice before taking leave is expected
    

## Other Employee Benefits

- Paid education/professional development (employer-funded courses)
    
- Public transport or gym membership discounts
    
- Novated lease (buy a car through company for tax benefits)
    

## Public Holidays

- Some are national; many are state-specific
    
- Example: Melbourne Cup Day is a Melbourne-only public holiday
    
- Full details: [fairwork.gov.au](https://www.fairwork.gov.au/)
    

## Salary

- **Minimum wage** reviewed annually; industry-specific — check [fairwork.gov.au](https://www.fairwork.gov.au/pay-and-wages)
    
- Salary negotiation: only after receiving an employment offer, and only if you can demonstrate value
    
- Pay frequency: fortnightly or monthly (employer-dependent)
    
- Pay slip provided each period: hours worked, tax deductions, net salary
    
- Tax rates: see [ATO income brackets](https://www.ato.gov.au/tax-rates-and-codes/tax-rates-australian-residents)
    

## Probation and Performance

- Standard probation: 3–6 months; employer may terminate if performance is unsatisfactory
    
- **Performance reviews** are common: regular discussions about performance, development goals
    
- Resigning: typically 2–4 weeks notice required; always check your contract
    

## Anti-Discrimination and Compliance

- Strong laws: equal employment opportunity, anti-discrimination
    
- Comments about appearance, gender, ethnicity, religion → potentially fireable
    
- Most employers provide free counseling services (psychological safety)

## Chapter 6 — Resume: Key Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not tailoring the resume** to the specific job — must use keywords from the job description and KSC
    
2. **Wrong format** — avoid multiple columns, graphics, tables, color; use **simple ATS-proof layout**
    
3. **Including personal information** — no photo, nationality, age, religion, political views
    
4. **Irrelevant work experience** — highlight only relevant roles or transferable skills
    
5. **Cramming information** — use bullet points, one sentence per task or achievement
    
6. **No LinkedIn profile or portfolio link** — especially important for designers, developers
    
7. **No achievements listed** — responsibilities alone are insufficient; quantify achievements where possible
    
8. **Buzzwords and clichés** — avoid "perfectionist," "hard worker," "team player" without evidence
    
9. **Lying or exaggerating** — will be caught in reference checks or on the job; avoid
    
10. **Unexplained employment gaps** — include at least one sentence explaining the gap + transferable skills gained
    
11. **Omitting international work experience** — international experience is valid and should be included if relevant
    
12. **Irrelevant hobbies** — only include extracurriculars relevant to the role or organization's values
    
13. **Google Translate copy-paste** — recruiters can identify machine-translated paragraphs; use translation for individual words only
    
14. **Not proofreading** — grammar/spelling mistakes signal lack of professionalism
    
15. **Resume too long** — 1–2 pages for most candidates; 3–4 pages only for very senior roles
    
16. **Including references on resume** — remove them; add "References available on request" if desired; references are only requested after interview

## Chapter 8 — Getting a Job with Little or No Experience

- **Highlight your education first** (not work experience) if you are a recent graduate
    
    - Include: qualifications, relevant subjects, final grades (if strong), thesis/research, academic distinctions
        
    - Include LinkedIn link with expanded education details
        
- **Highlight transferable skills** from: volunteering, sports clubs, university societies, casual work, retail, hospitality
    
- **Volunteer** in professional environments (HR, administration, marketing departments of nonprofits)
    
- **Do internships/placements** — check with your university; these may convert to permanent jobs
    
- **Network** with: teachers, fellow students, alumni portals, LinkedIn (recruiters and employers)
    
- **Freelance** to build a portfolio and client testimonials (Fiverr, Upwork)
    
- **Invest in education** — upskill via online courses if lacking specific skills

## Chapter 9 — Job Interview Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not researching the company** — know their services, values, culture before the interview
    
2. **Being unprepared** — practice answers using a structured approach; avoid going off-topic
    
3. **Downplaying your skills** — your skills are assets; use transferable skills if direct experience is limited
    
4. **Using clichés** — avoid "I'm a perfectionist/hard worker"; use real stories and examples instead
    
5. **Badmouthing previous employers, colleagues, or customers** — always remain professional
    
6. **Being too informal** — show personality but maintain professionalism
    
7. **Lying or exaggerating** — will surface in reference checks or probation
    
8. **Not asking questions at the end** — always prepare questions; shows interest and initiative
    
9. **Not knowing your expected salary** — research industry salary levels for your experience level beforehand
    
10. **Arriving late** — unacceptable; always plan extra commute time
    
11. **Dressing inappropriately** — match your clothing to the company's culture; research beforehand
    

> [!important] Interview Framework  
> Interviews are about **storytelling**. Every story has:
> 
> - A **beginning** (context/situation)
>     
> - A **middle** (the challenge/conflict)
>     
> - An **end** (result/learning)  
>     Use real examples. Avoid invented or exaggerated scenarios.
>

## [[KEY TAKEAWAYS]]

1. **Work rights are the gateway**: your visa determines everything — check conditions before applying.
    
2. **Local presence matters**: if you have a valid visa, move to Australia before job searching — an Australian address dramatically increases your response rate.
    
3. **Key Selection Criteria (KSC)** is the cornerstone of every Australian job application — tailor your resume, cover letter, and interview answers directly to the KSC.
    
4. **Australian resumes must be simple and ATS-compliant**: no photos, no personal information, no columns or graphics; 1–2 pages maximum.
    
5. **Superannuation is mandatory**: a % of your salary goes into a retirement fund; temporary residents can withdraw it before leaving.
    
6. **Australians value work-life balance genuinely**: 4 weeks paid leave, flexible hours, hybrid work, and psychological safety are standard expectations — not perks.
    
7. **Volunteering is the most reliable strategy** for breaking into the Australian job market with no local experience.
    
8. **Interviews are about storytelling**: use real, specific stories (situation → challenge → result); avoid clichés and generalizations.
    
9. **High-paying jobs do not always require a degree**: VET qualifications + industry experience open most paths.
    
10. **Cultural adaptation is non-negotiable**: punctuality, English-only in the workplace, no cheek-kissing, direct eye contact, and professionalism are minimum expectations.

## F. FAQ

> [!question] Q: Do I need IELTS to get a job in Australia?  
> **A:** Generally no. IELTS may be required for graduate programs at large consulting firms, but in most jobs it is not a requirement. Demonstrated English communication ability is more important.

> [!question] Q: Can I get a professional job without local Australian experience?  
> **A:** Yes, but it is more challenging. Strategies: volunteer in professional environments, do internships, highlight transferable skills, and network via LinkedIn and university alumni networks.

> [!question] Q: Should I include my international work experience on my Australian resume?  
> **A:** Yes, absolutely — if it is relevant to the role. International experience is valid and valued.

> [!question] Q: How long should an Australian resume be?  
> **A:** 1–2 pages for most candidates. 3–4 pages only for senior candidates with 10+ years of directly relevant experience.

> [!question] Q: Can I negotiate my salary in Australia?  
> **A:** Yes — but only after receiving a formal job offer. Research industry salary benchmarks for your skill level beforehand.

> [!question] Q: What happens if I apply from outside Australia?  
> **A:** You can apply, but response rates are significantly lower. If you have a valid visa, physically relocating before applying is strongly recommended.

## Industry Applications

- Skills in this video apply directly to: job searching, career transitions, integration as a migrant/student
    
- Relevant to all industries — the cultural and application skills are universal within Australia
    
- High-demand sectors noted: healthcare/disability support, cybersecurity, construction, digital marketing, recruitment
    

## Interview Prep

> [!important] Interview Alert  
> Australian interviews almost always use **behavioral questions** ("Tell me about a time when..."). Prepare 5–7 real stories from past experience mapped to common competencies: leadership, problem-solving, teamwork, communication, handling conflict, initiative.

- STAR method: **S**ituation → **T**ask → **A**ction → **R**esult
    
- Red flags: badmouthing past employers, vague answers without specific examples, arriving late, not researching the company
    

## Portfolio and Project Ideas

- Build a LinkedIn profile with a complete education and skills section
    
- Document volunteer work with outcomes (managed X social media accounts, grew following by Y%)
    
- Freelance small projects on Fiverr/Upwork to build a portfolio with real client testimonials
    

## Learning Path Connections

- **Prerequisites:** Basic English proficiency (B1 level minimum for workplace communication); understanding of resume writing
    
- **Next steps:** Practice STAR-method interview answers; deep-dive into [fairwork.gov.au](https://www.fairwork.gov.au/) for employment rights; research your specific industry on Seek
    
- **Advanced:** Enroll in a VET qualification relevant to your target industry; explore LinkedIn Learning for professional development
    

## Pro Tips

> [!tip] Pro Tip  
> The single highest-leverage action for someone new to Australia is volunteering in a professional environment. It provides local references (which employers value more than international ones), exposes you to Australian workplace norms, and often converts into paid employment. The video author credits this directly for getting her first professional job in Australia.

> [!tip] Pro Tip  
> Customize every single cover letter. Recruiters immediately identify generic letters. Reference the company's specific values, mission, or recent news to show genuine motivation — this differentiates you from the majority of applicants.
