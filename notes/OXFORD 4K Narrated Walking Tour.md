## [[NOTES]]

## Christ Church College

- One of the largest of Oxford's 39 colleges; founded in 1546 by King Henry VIII
    
- Home to ~600 students; notable alumni include Lewis Carroll, Albert Einstein (briefly, 1930s), and King Edward VII
    
- Produced 13 UK Prime Ministers — the highest of any single Oxford college
    
- Contains **Christ Church Cathedral**, one of the smallest cathedrals in Britain
    
    - Site originally a medieval priory founded in 1122
        
    - Taken over by Cardinal Wolsey in 1522; later by Henry VIII
        
    - The cathedral uniquely serves as the college chapel
        
- The **Tom Quad** (Great Quadrangle) is at the heart of Christ Church
    
    - During the English Civil War (1644–45), King Charles I made Oxford his wartime capital; Parliament sat in the Great Hall
        
    - Records of the Oxford Parliament were largely destroyed when Oliver Cromwell's forces captured the city in 1646
        

## Alice's Adventures in Wonderland — Oxford Connections

- **Alice's Shop** (across from Christ Church): a 15th-century building; inspired _The Old Sheep Shop_ in Lewis Carroll's _Through The Looking-Glass_
    
- The real **Alice Liddell** was the daughter of the Dean of Christ Church while Carroll lived here
    

> [!note] Literary Connection  
> Oxford served as the primary inspiration for both _Alice's Adventures in Wonderland_ and _Through The Looking-Glass_ by Lewis Carroll.

## Oxford's Geography and Origins

- Located in the heart of southern England, ~60 miles from London, Birmingham, and Bristol
    
- Originally settled by Anglo-Saxons; named **"Oxnaford"** — a ford (river crossing) for oxen
    
- Burned twice by Vikings; castle established by Normans in the late 11th century
    

## The Tom Tower and St. Aldate's Church

- **Tom Tower**: Built in 1682, designed by Christopher Wren (architect of St. Paul's Cathedral)
    
- Houses the bell **Great Tom**; rings 101 times every night at 9:05 PM
    
    - 101 = original 100 scholars at Christ Church's founding + 1 additional scholar ~120 years later
        
- **St. Aldate's Church**: Origins in the Anglo-Saxon period; current structure is a Victorian rebuild
    

## Carfax Tower

- 13th-century tower at the crossroads marking the centre of Oxford
    
- Once part of St. Martins' Church (long since demolished)
    
- Stands 74 ft (23 m) tall — legally the tallest permitted building in Oxford
    
- Rule has been broken on a few occasions in recent years
    

## The University of Oxford — Origins and Development

- Academic teaching believed to date back to **1096**
    
- Rapid development from **1167**: King Henry II banned English students from attending the University of Paris
    
- Official recognition via royal charter in **1248**
    
- The **"town and gown"** tensions (locals vs. students) prompted the building of independent halls of residence, which evolved into colleges
    

> [!important] Key Date  
> 1167 — King Henry II's ban on English students studying in Paris is considered the major catalyst for Oxford's rise as England's foremost centre of learning.

## The College System

- **Walter de Merton** (founder of Merton College, 1264) wrote statutes effectively creating a "constitution" for his college — turning dormitories into self-governing communities
    
- Students historically grouped into colleges by geographic origin (north vs. south England, Scotland); identities evolved over time
    
- Each college has its own coat of arms and unique traditions
    

## Oxford Covered Market and High Street

- Covered market built in the 1770s
    
- **Former All Saints' Church** (early 12th century origin): now serves as Lincoln College Library since 1975
    
- **Lincoln College**: Founded 1427
    
- **Brasenose College**: Founded 1509; named after a brass, nose-shaped door knocker; alumni include Michael Palin and David Cameron; home of one of the world's oldest rowing clubs (won world's first modern rowing race in 1815 vs. Jesus College)
    

## University Church of St. Mary the Virgin

- Largest parish church in Oxford; parts date from the 13th century
    
- Considered by some to be the first building of the University of Oxford
    
- Site of the **trial of the Oxford Martyrs**
    
- Hosted graduation ceremonies until the 17th century
    

## Magpie Lane

- Medieval alleyway; named after an alehouse
    
- Before the 17th century, known by a crude name (_Gropec**t Lane_) — a common naming convention across medieval England for streets with similar purposes
    
- Later renamed Grove Street; regained name Magpie Lane in **1927**
    
- Surrounded by Oriel College (founded 1324), Corpus Christi College (1517), and University College (1249 — claims to be the oldest college)
    

## Merton College

- Founded 1264; one of Oxford's three oldest colleges; largest in the dense university area
    
- Chapel's Gothic tower dates from 1450; interior among oldest and best-preserved in England
    
- **Time Ceremony**: Held every year since 1971; students walk backwards around the quad for one hour when clocks change from BST to GMT
    

## Examination Schools

- Opened 1882; one of the largest university buildings
    
- Where students take finals during **Trinity Term** (the last term of the academic year)
    
- Also hosts the annual **Freshers' Fair** during Michaelmas Term
    

## Magdalen College

- Founded 1458; considered one of the most beautiful of the 39 colleges
    
- Iconic Tudor-period tower — centrepoint of the **May Morning** tradition
    
    - Choir sings a hymn from the tower at 6am on 1st May every year; has taken place for over 500 years

## All Souls College

- No undergraduate students; functions as a research institute since the late 17th century
    
- Serves as a reminder that academia is about more than teaching — it creates new knowledge
    

## Radcliffe Camera

- Built in the late 1740s; originally an independent library
    
- Absorbed into the university in 1927; now an additional reading room for the **Bodleian Library**
    
- Named after **Dr. John Radcliffe**, royal physician to William III and Mary II; donated £40,000 in his 1714 will for its construction
    

## The Bodleian Library

- One of the oldest working libraries in Europe; in operation since **1602**
    
- Re-founded by **Sir Thomas Bodley**, a former fellow of Merton College
    
- Over **13 million printed items**; second largest library in the UK after the British Library
    
- **Not a lending library** — books may only be read on premises; famously refused King Charles I permission to borrow a book in 1645
    

## Bridge of Sighs (Hertford Bridge)

- Built 1914; links two parts of Hertford College
    
- Named after its visual similarity to the Bridge of Sighs in Venice
    

## Clarendon Building and Sheldonian Theatre

- **Clarendon Building** (opened 1715): originally the home of **Oxford University Press**, the world's largest university printing press; now part of the Bodleian
    
- **Sheldonian Theatre** (built 1669): designed by Christopher Wren; used for grand ceremonies and graduations
    
    - University press originally worked in its basement but had to move out due to noise — leading to the construction of the Clarendon Building
        

## Harry Potter Scar

- A stonecutter's signature mark on the ground beside the Sheldonian Theatre
    
- Said to be the inspiration for **Harry Potter's scar**: J.K. Rowling reportedly saw it during a visit to Oxford and used it as the visual model
    

## Broad Street — Inspector Morse and the White Horse Pub

- **The White Horse Pub**: One of Oxford's oldest pubs, dating to 1591; a favoured location in the TV series _Inspector Morse_ (ITV, 1987–2000)
    
- Based on novels by **Colin Dexter**; Morse is a detective in the Oxford constabulary known for refined tastes
    

## Balliol College

- Founded 1263; alumni include four British Prime Ministers: HH Asquith, Harold Macmillan, Edward Heath, Boris Johnson
    

## The Oxford Martyrs

- Three Protestant bishops tried at the University Church in 1555 under the Catholic Queen Mary I (daughter of Henry VIII)
    
- **Burnt at the stake** on Broad Street, including **Thomas Cranmer**, the acting Archbishop of Canterbury
    
- A major flashpoint during Queen Mary's reign
    

## Church of St. Mary Magdalen

- Origins outside the old North Gate; current building dates to the late 12th century
    
- Name pronounced "Magdalene" — differs from **Magdalen College** (pronounced "Maudelin") due to shifts in pronunciation over centuries; the college retained its older pronunciation
    

## Martyrs' Memorial

- Erected in 1843 (~300 years after the Martyrs' execution)
    
- Located at what was then the edge of Oxford's medieval city walls
    

## Beaumont Street and the Ashmolean Museum

- **Beaumont Palace** (long since demolished): birthplace of Kings Richard I and John
    
- **Ashmolean Museum** (founded 1683): first public museum in Britain; exhibited curiosities including coins, engravings, and the last dodo seen in Europe; donated by **Elias Ashmole**
    
- **The Randolph Hotel**: Victorian era; regularly featured in _Inspector Morse_; now houses a "Morse Bar"
    

## Cornmarket Street

- Oldest building in Oxford: **St Michael at the North Gate Church tower**, built in **1040**
    
- Corn market took place here from the medieval period; lead roof demolished in 1644 for Civil War bullets
    
- Originally named Northgate Street
    

## St. Scholastica Day Riot (1355)

- Began on 10th February at the **Swindlestock Tavern on Carfax** — two students complained about wine quality
    
- Escalated from a brawl to **three days of rioting**; 93 people killed
    
- A flashpoint in the long-standing town-and-gown tensions
    

## Origins of the University of Cambridge

- Roughly 150 years before the St. Scholastica Riots, two students were lynched by Oxford locals
    
- A group of Oxford scholars fled to **Cambridge**, founding what became the University of Cambridge — the second-oldest university in the English-speaking world
    

> [!note] Oxford–Cambridge Rivalry  
> Oxford students refer to Cambridge as "The Other Place" — a custom reciprocated in Cambridge. The rivalry culminates annually in the **University Boat Race** on the River Thames in London.

## Queen Street

- Originally known as **The Bailey** (linked to the castle); later **Butcher Row** (meat market)
    
- Renamed Queen Street after **Queen Charlotte**, wife of King George III, who visited Oxford in 1785
    
- **Bonn Square**: named after Oxford's sister city, Bonn, Germany; site of a former church graveyard; contains the **Tirah Memorial**, the first war memorial dedicated in Oxford
    
- **Westgate Shopping Centre**: originally opened 1972; massively refurbished 2017
    

## Oxford Castle

- Built on orders of William the Conqueror in **1071**; one of the largest motte-and-bailey castles in England at the time
    
- Much of it destroyed during the English Civil War; subsequently used as a prison
    
- **Oxford Prison**: operational until **1996**; now a hotel
    
- Notable structures:
    
    - **18th-century Prison Debtors' Tower**
        
    - **St. George's Tower**: oldest part of the castle complex; one of the oldest pieces of military architecture in England
        
- Key conflicts: Siege of Oxford (1142), First Barons' War, English Civil War (Royalists based here 1642–1646; city captured by Parliamentarians after a two-month siege in 1646)

## [[KEY TAKEAWAYS]]

1. Oxford is the home of the oldest university in the English-speaking world, with academic teaching dating back to approximately 1096.
    
2. The University of Oxford's rapid growth was directly triggered by King Henry II's 1167 ban on English students attending the University of Paris.
    
3. Merton College (founded 1264) set the template for all Oxford colleges by introducing written statutes, transforming student housing into self-governing academic communities.
    
4. The long-standing "town and gown" conflict between Oxford locals and university students directly led to the founding of the University of Cambridge, when fleeing Oxford scholars established a new institution there.
    
5. The Bodleian Library — one of Europe's oldest working libraries, with over 13 million items — famously does not lend books, a rule so strict it even refused King Charles I.
    
6. Oxford's architecture is dominated by university buildings and churches, giving rise to its nickname **"The City of Dreaming Spires"**.
    
7. Oxford has deep literary connections: it inspired _Alice's Adventures in Wonderland_, _Through The Looking-Glass_, the _Inspector Morse_ TV series, and allegedly the visual design of Harry Potter's scar.
    
8. Oxford Castle, built in 1071, served as a military fortress, a Civil War headquarters for the Royalists, and later a prison until 1996.

## C. SIMPLIFIED RE-EXPLANATION

**Plain Language (ELI5):**  
Oxford is a city in England where a very old and very famous university has existed for nearly 1,000 years. The university isn't one building — it's 39 separate "colleges," each like a mini school with its own rules, history, and personality. The city grew up around the university, which caused lots of fights between students and local people over the centuries. Those fights, and other events like wars and religious changes, shaped almost every building you see in Oxford today.

**Analogy:**  
Think of the University of Oxford like a city within a city. The 39 colleges are like separate neighbourhoods, each with their own mayor (the Head of College), their own residents (students and fellows), and their own culture. The wider university is like the city government — it sets overarching rules, runs exams, and awards degrees, but the neighbourhoods run their own daily lives.

## F. FAQ — FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

> [!question] Q: What is the difference between the University of Oxford and its colleges?  
> **A:** The University of Oxford is the overarching institution that awards degrees and sets academic standards. The 39 colleges are self-governing communities within it — each with its own buildings, governance, traditions, and identity. Students belong to both their college and the university.

> [!question] Q: Why does Oxford have so many colleges?  
> **A:** The college system evolved from medieval halls of residence built to house students separately from local townspeople, reducing "town and gown" tensions. Merton College's statutes (1264) formalised the model of self-governing communities, which all later colleges followed.

> [!question] Q: How is Magdalen College pronounced?  
> **A:** "Maudelin" — reflecting a pronunciation common when the college was founded, which has since become archaic. The nearby Church of St. Mary Magdalen uses the modern pronunciation "Magdalene."

> [!question] Q: Why did Cambridge University exist?  
> **A:** Oxford scholars fled to Cambridge after violent anti-student riots in Oxford (c. 1205), establishing what became the University of Cambridge — the second-oldest university in the English-speaking world.

> [!question] Q: Is the Bodleian Library really that strict about lending?  
> **A:** Yes. It refused even King Charles I when he requested to borrow a book in 1645. The rule has been in place since the library's founding and remains in force today.
