## Core facts about AI and exam prep

- AI (ChatGPT, Copilot, etc.) can generate realistic Cambridge‑style tasks for exam format, vocabulary lists, writing prompts, listening texts, reading/use‑of‑English tasks, and speaking roleplays.
    
- AI sometimes “hallucinates”: it invents explanations, rules, or even words that are not in the text, while sounding very confident.
    
- To catch AI’s mistakes, you already need enough knowledge to suspect something is wrong; otherwise you will accept incorrect explanations.
    
- Free models may not be fully up to date with recent exam changes; paid versions are more likely to have current information.
    
- AI is especially unreliable on more complex, constrained tasks (e.g. sentence transformations with multiple correct answers, subtle grammar explanations, precise application of Cambridge assessment criteria).
    
- Speaking and writing are the areas where AI feedback is currently weakest, and where a human teacher and a structured course are still considered essential.
    

## Key analogies and metaphors

- AI should be “a tool in your toolbox, like a good dictionary or app,” supporting your learning but not leading it.
    
- “If you fail to plan, you plan to fail” and the axe analogy: if you have limited time to cut down a tree, you should spend most of it sharpening the axe, used to justify heavy planning before writing.
    
- “Burying my head in the sand”: ignoring AI doesn’t make it go away; you must learn to use it rather than pretend it doesn’t exist.
    

## Actionable steps the video recommends

## 1. Use AI to understand the exam and plan study

- Prompt AI for an overview of your specific exam (B2 First, C1 Advanced, C2 Proficiency), including parts, timings, and task types.
    
- Ask AI to list topic areas and then 20 common words/expressions for a specific topic (e.g. “communication and media”), then optionally get translations and example sentences.
    
- Put unfamiliar words into a spaced‑repetition app (e.g. Anki) and review them in short sessions.
    

## 2. Use AI for writing practice in a controlled way

- Ask AI to generate a realistic writing task for your exam level.
    
- First, plan the essay yourself (5–15 minutes) and write the full answer without any AI help, as in the real exam.
    
- Only after writing, ask AI to create a brief outline for the same question and compare your plan with its plan to see what you missed.
    
- Optionally, ask AI to turn its plan into a full sample essay, then compare structure, content, and language with your own work.
    
- Paste your own essay into AI and explicitly ask for feedback using Cambridge criteria (content, communicative achievement, organization, language), but treat the feedback as helpful, not infallible.
    

## 3. Use AI + other tools for listening

- Ask AI to create a listening task (e.g. B2 First Part 2 sentence‑completion), including questions, answers, and a script.
    
- Paste the script into a text‑to‑speech tool (e.g. Eleven Labs), choose a natural‑sounding voice, generate audio, and then do the listening task yourself.
    
- Use these tasks as extra practice but do not rely 100% on the AI‑generated answers being correct.
    

## 4. Use AI for Reading & Use of English, with caution

- Ask AI to create typical Reading and Use of English tasks (e.g. open cloze) with answers.
    
- Be aware that in real Cambridge tasks there can be multiple correct answers; AI often gives only one, so do not assume alternatives are wrong.
    
- For complex sentence transformations, prefer real Cambridge sample papers; AI‑made versions can be useful drills but often do not fully match exam format.
    

## 5. Use AI for speaking roleplays – but not as your only “teacher”

- Use prompts to set up realistic speaking tasks (e.g. C1 Advanced Speaking Part 3) where AI plays another candidate or the examiner and gives instructions.
    
- Optionally use voice‑enabled systems (e.g. Copilot, Sesame) to practice speaking in a more natural, interactive way.
    
- Add a prompt asking AI to correct your vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation during the conversation, but recognize that its correction is partial and often misses errors.
    
- After a Part 3‑style discussion, prompt AI to switch roles and act as the examiner for Part 4 follow‑up questions.
    

## 6. Structural “rules” for using AI overall

- Treat AI as a support tool, not the main driver of your learning or preparation.
    
- Do the core cognitive work (planning, writing, speaking, exam decisions) yourself first, then use AI to compare, refine, and extend.
    
- Always cross‑check AI output against real exam samples and, when possible, with a human teacher—especially for writing and speaking.
