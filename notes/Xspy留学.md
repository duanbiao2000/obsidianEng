**Work**

_Have you found anything interesting in your work recently?_

> "Actually yes — I've been experimenting with AI tools to automate some of the more repetitive parts of my job, and it's been a bit of a rabbit hole honestly. What surprised me is how much time I was wasting on things that can now be done in seconds. It's slightly unsettling but mostly fascinating."

_What do you dislike most about working?_

> "Probably the context-switching — jumping between tasks constantly without ever getting into a real flow state. I find it genuinely draining. I'd much rather go deep on one thing than skim across ten."


**Study**

_Have you learned anything interesting recently in your studies?_

> "I've been going down a rabbit hole on behavioral economics — specifically how people make irrational decisions in predictable ways. It sounds dry but once you see the patterns, you notice them everywhere, in pricing, in politics, in your own choices."

_What do you dislike most about studying?_

> "The gap between understanding something and actually being able to use it. You can read about a concept, feel like you've got it, then completely freeze when it comes to applying it. That frustration never really goes away."


**Where you live**

_Where do you live, in an apartment or a house?_

> "An apartment, right in the city center. It's compact but the location makes up for it — everything I need is within walking distance, which I've come to value more than space honestly."

_How long have you lived in your area?_

> "About three years now. ==Long enough to have my routines down== but I still occasionally discover a street or a café I hadn't noticed before, which I like."

_What do you like about the area you're living in?_

> "The energy mostly. It's dense and a bit chaotic but there's always something happening. I think I'd go a bit ==stir-crazy== somewhere too quiet."


**Why these answers score 8.0+**

|What examiners notice|How it's applied above|
|---|---|
|Natural hedging language|"honestly," "mostly," "a bit of"|
|Self-correction / spontaneity|"It sounds dry but..."|
|Idiomatic phrases|"rabbit hole," "flow state," "stir-crazy"|
|Extended response without rambling|Each answer is 2-3 sentences with a clear arc|
|Avoiding generic answers|No "I like my area because it is convenient" flatness|

**One rule to remember:** never just answer the question — **answer + reason + personal reaction**. That three-part structure naturally produces Band 8 responses.

**Museums**

_Do you think more museums should be built in your city?_

> "I'm somewhat torn on this. More museums sounds great in principle, but if they're underfunded and half-empty, they're just expensive buildings. I'd rather see existing ones improved — better curation, more interactive exhibits — than a proliferation of mediocre new ones."

_Did you often go to museums when you were a child?_

> "Occasionally, usually on school trips rather than by choice. I remember finding them a bit static and overwhelming at that age. It wasn't until I was older that I started actually engaging with what I was looking at."

_Do you like going to museums with your friends?_

> "It depends entirely on the friend. With the right person it becomes a conversation — you're bouncing reactions off each other, debating interpretations. With the wrong person you're just being rushed through rooms. So yes, selectively."

**Happy Things**

_What usually makes you feel happy?_

> "==Small things disproportionately== — a good coffee in the morning, finishing something I'd been procrastinating on, an unexpected message from someone I haven't spoken to in a while. I've stopped waiting for big events to feel good."

_Do you like sharing photos when you're happy?_

> "Not really. I tend to be quite private about moments that actually matter to me. There's something about posting happiness publicly that feels like it slightly dilutes it. I'd rather just sit in it."

_Who do you like to share your happiness with?_

> "A pretty small circle — maybe two or three people who genuinely get what matters to me. Quality over quantity, definitely."

_Do you like hearing good news more or bad news more?_

> "Good news, obviously — but bad news first if I have to choose between receiving both. I'd rather process the hard thing and then have something to look forward to."

**Shoes**

_Do you like buying shoes? How often?_

> "Not particularly. I'm a function-over-form person. I probably buy two or three pairs a year — when something wears out rather than as a recreational activity."

_Have you ever bought shoes online?_

> "Yes, and ==I've had mixed results==. The sizing inconsistency across brands is genuinely ==maddening==. I've started only buying online from brands I already know fit me well."

_How much money do you usually spend on shoes?_

> "I tend to invest in one decent pair rather than several cheap ones. ==False economy ==otherwise — cheap shoes ==fall apart== and hurt your feet. Somewhere in the mid-range, not luxury but ==not bargain bin==."

_Which do you prefer, fashionable or comfortable shoes?_

> "Comfortable, ==without hesitation==. Though ==I'd push back slightly on the premise== — I think well-designed shoes can be both. ==The false choice between looking good and feeling good is mostly a marketing problem.=="

**Rules**

_Are there any rules for students in your school?_

> "The usual ones — attendance, dress code, phone policies. ==The ones that made sense were easy to follow== . The ones that felt arbitrary were universally ignored, ==which probably says something about how rules should be designed==."

_Did you have any strict teachers when you were a child?_

> "One or two. ==Interestingly==, ==the ones I remember most fondly== were often the stricter ones — they had high expectations and you could feel it. ==Strictness without care is just unpleasant, but strictness with care is motivating==."

_Do you think schools should have more rules or fewer rules?_

> "Fewer, but better enforced and better explained. The problem isn't usually the number of rules — it's rules that exist for administrative convenience rather than actual benefit to students. Less quantity, more clarity and buy-in."

**Fruit and Vegetables**

_Are you interested in growing fruits and vegetables?_

> "More than I expected to be, honestly. I tried growing herbs on my windowsill and found the whole process oddly satisfying — ==there's something grounding about it that screens and work can't replicate==."

_Do you think plants are easy to grow?_

> "Depends entirely on the plant and the person. Herbs are forgiving. Tomatoes will humble you. I think most people overestimate what's required and underestimate how much they'd enjoy it."

_Is the place where you live suitable for growing fruits and vegetables?_

> "Not ideally — apartment living with limited sunlight. ==But I've made it work with containers and a bit of creativity==. You'd be surprised what you can grow in a small space with the right setup."

_Do you watch TV programs about fruits and vegetables?_

> "Not specifically, but I've fallen down YouTube rabbit holes about ==urban farming and permaculture==. ==It scratches a similar itch without the commitment of an actual allotment==."

**Spending Time with Elders**

_Who are the elders you usually spend time with?_

> "Mainly my grandparents ==when I visit family==. There's ==a quality of conversation== you can only get from someone who's lived through genuinely different eras — the perspective shift is valuable in a way that peers can't really offer."


_What do you think ==are the benefits of spending time with elders?==_

> "Perspective, mainly. ==People who've lived through genuinely different circumstances have a kind of pattern recognition you simply can't get from peers==. ==They've already made the mistakes you're about to make==. Whether you actually listen is another question."

_Have you recently spent time with an elder?_

> "Yes, over the holidays with my grandparents. What struck me was ==how unhurried the conversations felt== — no one checking their phone, ==no agenda==. It's a pace I don't naturally maintain ==but genuinely appreciate when I'm in it==."

**Public Places**

_Have you ever talked to a stranger in a public place?_

> "Occasionally, and it's almost always been ==unexpectedly== pleasant. There's something freeing about a conversation with no history and no future — you're just two people briefly ==occupying the same space==. I think we've ==collectively== lost some appetite for that."

_Do you usually wear headphones in public places?_

> "Honestly, yes — ==and I'm slightly conflicted about it==. They're useful for focus but they're also ==a very effective social barrier==. ==I've started leaving one ear free as a small compromise.=="

_Do you think we need more public places?_

> "Definitely. Not just physically more, but better designed ones — places that encourage people to actually linger and interact rather than pass through. ==A lot of urban planning optimizes for movement rather than presence.=="

**Crowded Places**

_Where in your city are there usually a lot of people?_

> "Transport hubs and commercial streets on weekends mostly. The density is manageable if you're moving with it, ==but the moment you're trying to go against the flow it becomes genuinely exhausting==."

_When was the last time you went to a crowded place?_

> "Last weekend at a market. I went early specifically to avoid the worst of it, which tells you something about my relationship with crowds."

_Do you like staying in crowded places?_

> "Not for ==extended periods==. I can tolerate it, even enjoy the energy briefly, ==but I hit a ceiling pretty quickly. I think I recharge in quiet more than most people.=="

_How do you feel when you're in a crowded place?_

> "==Mildly overstimulated==. ==There's an initial buzz and then a slow drain==. ==I find myself people-watching more than participating==, which is its own kind of enjoyment — just a more passive one."

**Advertisement**

_Do you often see advertisements on your phone or computer?_

> "Constantly, though I've become somewhat ==desensitized==. The ones that catch me are the ==eerily== ==well-targeted ones== — that moment of 'how did it know?' is unsettling and impressive ==in equal measure==."

_What is the most impressive advertisement you've seen?_

> "There was a campaign ==years ago== that told a complete emotional story ==in under a minute== — no product shown until the last second. I remembered the feeling long after I forgot what it was selling, which is either brilliant or a complete failure ==depending on how you look at it==."

_Are there a lot of advertisements around you?_

> "Everywhere, almost inescapably. What's changed is they've become ambient — woven into content rather than interrupting it, which I'd argue makes them more insidious, not less."

_Do you think advertisements are useful?_

> "They can be — ==genuinely introducing you to something you'd have wanted anyway==. ==But most are just noise competing for attention==. ==The ratio of useful to intrusive is not flattering for the industry.=="

**Borrow**

_Do you borrow books from your friends?_

> "Rarely borrow, but I give them away fairly often. Books feel like they want to keep moving — I'd rather pass one on than have it sit on a shelf unread."

_Do you lend things to others?_

> "Selectively. ==I've learned the hard way that some things don't come back==, and more importantly, some relationships change slightly when borrowing enters them. I lend ==freely== to people I trust completely."

_Will you lend your mobile phone to others?_

> "In an emergency, yes without hesitation. Casually, I'd be a bit reluctant — not because of trust exactly, but phones have become so personal that handing one over ==feels oddly exposing==."

_What do you think about borrowing money from friends?_

> "==It almost always introduces friction that wasn't there before==. Even with the best intentions on both sides, there's a power imbalance that's hard to ignore. I'd rather find another solution and keep the friendship clean."

**Sharing**

_Did your parents teach you to share when you were a child?_

> "In the standard ways — sharing toys, taking turns. ==What they modeled more than taught== was sharing time and attention, which I think matters more in the long run."

_What kind of things do you like to share with others?_

> "Ideas and recommendations mostly — a good book, a podcast, ==something I read that reframed how I think about something==. ==Material things less so. I find sharing something that genuinely changed my perspective feels more meaningful than splitting a meal.=="
