## Core Exercise: Retelling Stories

- One exercise can 5x–10x speaking ability while also teaching new vocabulary, grammar, and “cool things,” and it is scientifically shown to improve speaking.​
- The exercise can be done anywhere, anytime, alone and for free, without tutors or classes, but few people do it because it is uncomfortable and requires discipline.​
- The exercise: **retelling stories** — pick a story, read or listen to it, then tell that story back to someone else or to yourself.​
- Very few people do it because:
    - It feels unpleasant to do alone.
    - It is not comfortable and pushes you out of your comfort zone.
    - It requires self-discipline.​

> [!note] Core Definition
> Retelling stories = consume a story (reading or listening), then immediately tell the same story back using its vocabulary, phrases, and structures in your own words.​

## Why Retelling Stories Works

- In language learning theory, there are two extremes: **input-based** and **output-based** approaches.​
- **Input-based theory**:
    - Focuses on comprehensible input and immersion.
    - You mostly listen and read.
    - You do almost no output, no active speaking practice.​
- **Output-based theory**:
    - Focuses on practicing speaking in social settings.
    - Uses the fact that language is a social tool.
    - You get benefits from using language in social environments.​
- Both extremes have benefits and drawbacks, but people tend to choose one and attack the other (pure input vs. pure output).​
- Some say: only comprehensible input, forget speaking/output until language is strong (often thousands of hours).​
- Others say: only active speaking, only practice with others, comprehension is not the priority.​
- Best results come from **combining input and output**, and retelling stories is designed to do exactly that.​

> [!important] Combined Input + Output
> Retelling forces you to first comprehend (input), then immediately produce (output) using the same vocabulary, phrases, and grammar, which makes the language stick better long-term.​

- Process:
    - First you read or listen.
    - Then you retell the same story using newly learned words, phrases, and grammar structures.​
- Comprehension alone:
    - Has benefits but takes a lot of time for the brain to truly learn the language if not followed by active usage.​
- If you:
    - Comprehend first, then immediately practice all new vocabulary and structures,
    - Words tend to stick better and longer.​
- You still need spaced repetition and classic learning techniques, but using new words immediately increases:  
    - Long-term retention.
    - Ease of recall when needed.​
- Retelling, by definition, is one of the best exercises to practice input and output together in one activity, combining multiple benefits.​

## Impact on Speaking Overall (Even in Native Language)

- The exercise improves speech in general, regardless of whether you use your target language or your native language.​
- Benefits include:
    - Structured speaking.
    - Vocal delivery.
    - Tone.
    - Storytelling ability.​
- So even if you retell in your native language, you still train overall speaking and presentation skills.​

## How to Do the Exercise Correctly (Checklist)

- Short checklist to do the exercise properly; it varies slightly with your English level but is mostly universal.​

> [!note] Main Checklist
>
> 1. Pick a slightly challenging text (5–10 new items).​
>     
> 2. Read the text.​
>     
> 3. Highlight everything you do not know.​
>     
> 4. Retell the story using all highlighted items.​
>     
> 5. Assess performance.​
>     
> 6. Repeat once if not satisfied and then move on.​
>     

## 1. Pick a Slightly Challenging Text

- Choose a text that is **slightly above your current level**.​
- For the speaker, this means: 5–10 new words, new phrases, or new grammar structures per text — 5–10 new **elements** total.​
- This range is the “sweet spot”:
    - If you go above it, it becomes hard for the brain to learn everything.
    - With 5–10 new things, you have much better chances to remember them.​
- Example: If you are B2 level, pick a text at upper B2 or lower C1; many online resources make this easy.​

## 2. Read (or Listen to) the Text

- Recommendation: read the text (simpler to manage resources).​
- You can also practice listening, but:
    - Harder to find appropriate listening resources.
    - Makes the setup more complicated.​
- Still, listening is possible if you prefer it.​

## 3. Highlight Unknown Items

- While reading, highlight everything you do not know:
    - New words.
    - New phrases.
    - New grammar structures.​
- Important that highlights are **visible** after reading.​
- Once finished reading and highlighting, your speaking goal will be to use **all** the newly highlighted items while retelling.​
- Key reason: instead of only loading new items into **passive vocabulary**, you:
    - Train your brain to use them in different contexts.
    - Load them into **active vocabulary**, which is what you need in speaking.​

## 4. First Retelling and Performance Focus

- After reading and highlighting, you retell the story based on the text.​
- Then you **assess your performance**.​
- For beginners and intermediate learners:
    - Cut yourself some slack.
    - Do not overthink every word or every grammar structure.​
    - Focus on continuity and getting the story across, not on details.​
    - Main goal: develop confidence first.​
- For advanced and fluent speakers:
    - Pay more attention to how you are speaking.​
    - Check:
        - Speed of delivery.
        - Overall delivery quality.
        - If you were giving a presentation with the same story, would it be engaging and interesting for the audience?​
    - Think about:
        - Words used.
        - Sentence formation speed.
        - Grammar correctness.
        - Building good speaking habits, avoiding frequent mistakes.​

## 5. Going One Step Further: Second Attempt

- Most people stop after telling the story once and doing a quick “good/bad” self-judgment.​
- Recommended: go **one step further**.​
- If you did not like your performance (for any level), and it feels below your current level:
    - Example: you are a C1 speaker, but you spoke like B1/B2.​
- Next step: **repeat the same exercise with the same text a second time**.​
- On the second attempt:
    - You already know all the words.
    - You know the text structure.
    - You do not worry about what to say next.
    - You can focus all attention on **speech quality**:
        - Reducing filler phrases (“you know,” “like,” etc.).
        - Improving pace.
        - Making speech more continuous and fluent.​
- If still not satisfied after the second attempt:
    - Do **not** go for a third retelling.
    - Instead, pick another text and practice various other scenarios.​

## Full Checklist Recap

- Pick a text slightly above your level (5–10 new elements).​
- Read the text.​
- Highlight everything you do not know.​
- Retell the story using all new words/phrases/structures.​
- Assess performance and how appropriate it is for your level.​
- If not satisfied, repeat once with the same text and then move on.​

## Making the Exercise More Effective

- Three main ways to modify/extend the exercise:​
    
    1. Change the narrative.​
    2. Change your performance.​
    3. Tell a similar story.​

## 1. Changing the Narrative

- For beginners and intermediates:
    - Change **time** of the story (present → past or future).​
    - Forces use of past tense or future tense structures.​
    - Trains ability to talk about the same story in different time frames.​
- For more advanced learners:
    - Tell the **same story three times** with different audience assumptions:​
        
        1. As if talking to a **six-year-old child**:
            
            - Use very simple language and structures.
            - Practice **oversimplifying** instead of overcomplicating.​
                
        2. As if in a **job meeting**, giving a presentation to colleagues:
            
            - Use more professional, formal language.
            - More structured story.
            - Use many transitional words and phrases: “however,” “in addition to that,” “on one hand,” “on the other hand,” etc.​
            - Targets professional speaking abilities.​
                
        3. As if you were a **scholar or philosopher** talking to another scholar:
            
            - More creative and less practical, but fun.​
            - Imagine you were Jordan Peterson explaining the story.
            - Use more abstract, convoluted, and complicated wording.
            - Tone and speaking manner are different.
            - Practice **overcomplicating** the story.​
- Summary: four narrative modifications:
    - Change the time of the story.
    - Change who you are speaking to: child, colleagues, scholars.​

## 2. Changing Your Performance

- Add constraints on **how** you speak.​

## 2.1 Time Constraints (10–30–60 seconds)

- After reading the story:
    - Set a timer for **10 seconds**, tell the same story in 10 seconds:
        - Go straight to the point.
        - Focus on the gist, minimal context.​
    - Then tell it in **30 seconds**:
        - Slightly more detail, still limited.​
    - Then tell it in **60 seconds** (maximum):
        - More space to add detail.
        - Still get to the point quickly.​
- This trains:
    - Prioritization: deciding what to say and what to skip.
    - Pacing: speaking speed and rhythm.​

## 2.2 Improving Performance Directly (Speed + Quality)

- Give yourself **two attempts** with timing:​
    - First attempt:
        - Start speaking.
        - Use a stopwatch to measure total time for the story.​
    - Second attempt:
        - Take a short break.
        - Tell the same story again, but aim to be **twice as fast**.​
        - Example: if first time took 2 minutes, aim for under 1 minute.​
- During the second attempt, focus on:
    - Eliminating filler words (“you know,” “like,” etc.).​
    - Forming sentences faster.​
    - Applying grammar structures faster and more automatically.​
- It is easier the second time because:
    - Vocabulary is already activated.
    - You know the story structure.
    - This solidifies vocabulary, grammar, and fluency together.​

## 3. Telling a Similar Story

- This is almost a separate exercise but closely related and closest to real conversation.​
- It improves:
    - Speed of sentence formation.
    - Speed of finding words.
    - General speaking ability.
    - Ability to always know what to talk about and how to respond.​
- Process:
    - Read a text (e.g., a non-fiction scientific paper).​
    - After retelling the original story, your goal is to come up with a **similar story**.​
    - Ask: what does this story remind you of?​
    - Simulate a conversation:
        - Someone tells you about this research.
        - You respond with a story about something similar.​
- Example in the video:
    - Original paper: about people being overconfident in their judgment and putting too much belief in others compared to actual ability.​
    - Similar story: the Dunning–Kruger effect.​
    - You tell the story of the Dunning–Kruger effect in response.​
- This exercise is hardest because:
    - You choose the content yourself.
    - You do **not** have the original text to reference.
    - You lack full details; you must recall from your own life, experiences, or previous reading.​
- Recommended especially for **fluent speakers**:
    - If previous variations are easy, focus on:
        - Reading a story.
        - Giving your opinion.
        - Responding with a similar story or personal experience.​
    - This directly translates to real-life conversations and helps avoid “blank stare” moments where you do not know what to say.​

## Consistency, Volume, and Social Aspect

- Most important factor is **not** which variation you pick but:
    - How often you do the exercise.
    - How consistent you are.
    - How much volume you accumulate.​
- Doing the exercise only once or twice will have no profound effect on language or speaking skills because the volume is too low.​

> [!important] Quantifiable Goal
> Set a measurable target: total hours or total number of texts retold (e.g., 30–50 hours, 50–100 texts), and work toward it consistently.​

- Suggested quantifiable goals:
    - Time-based: e.g., 30 hours or 50 hours of retelling stories.​
    - Count-based: e.g., retell 50 texts or 100 texts.​
- More repetitions and texts = greater effect on language and speaking.​
- Any exercise needs consistency to have effect; this one is no exception.​

## Doing It With a Partner

- Best way to be consistent: find someone to do the exercise with.​
- Research suggests:
    - Because language is a **social tool**, we learn it best in social environments.​
    - Doing this with another person may yield better results than doing it alone.​
- A partner adds **accountability**:
    - You feel responsible to show up because you owe it to them, not just yourself.​

## Closing Remarks

## [[KEY TAKEAWAYS]]

1. Retelling stories is a single, high-impact exercise that combines input and output to improve speaking, vocabulary, grammar, and storytelling all at once.​
2. The ideal text difficulty is slightly above your level, with about 5–10 new words, phrases, or structures per text for optimal learning.​
3. Highlighting unknown items and forcing yourself to use them in your retelling moves them from passive to active vocabulary.​
4. Beginners should focus on continuity and confidence, while advanced speakers should focus on delivery quality, speed, and accuracy.​
5. Modifying the exercise by changing narrative (time, audience), performance (time limits, speed), or telling similar stories allows you to target different speaking skills.​
6. The “similar story” variation is closest to real conversation and trains you to always have something relevant to say.​
7. Consistency and volume—measured in total hours or number of texts retold—matter more than which exact variation you choose.​
8. Doing the exercise with a partner leverages the social nature of language and adds accountability, making long-term practice more likely.​

## A. CORE QUESTIONS

**What is this?**
A structured speaking exercise where you read or listen to a story, highlight unknown language, and then retell the story using all the new vocabulary, phrases, and grammar patterns.​

**Why is this important?**
It combines input and output in one activity, making new language stick faster and longer while also improving general speaking, delivery, and storytelling skills.​

**Why should I learn this?**
It is low-cost, flexible, and can be done alone or with a partner, giving you a repeatable system to steadily grow fluency and confidence without relying on classes.​

**When will I need this?**
Whenever you want faster progress in speaking—before presentations, interviews, exams, or when you want to turn reading/listening into speaking practice.​

**How does this work?**
You process content deeply (through reading/listening and highlighting), then immediately force your brain to recall and use new language, which cements it into active vocabulary.​

**What problem does this solve?**
It bridges the gap between silent input-only learning and chaotic, unstructured speaking practice, giving you a controlled but realistic way to train speaking.​

## C. SIMPLIFIED RE-EXPLANATION

**ELI5 version**:
You first listen to a story and mark the words you do not know. Then you try to tell the same story yourself using those new words. Doing this many times makes your speaking faster and clearer because your brain gets used to using those words, not just seeing them.​

**Analogy**:
It is like watching a move in chess, then immediately trying to replay that move yourself on the board. Just watching teaches you a bit, but replaying fixes the move in your hands and memory.​

**Step-by-step in plain language**:

1. Choose a text that is a little difficult but not too hard.​
2. Read it and highlight everything new.​
3. Tell the story using all highlighted parts, without reading.​
4. Check how well you spoke (flow, confidence, mistakes).​
5. If unhappy, repeat once; if okay, move to a new story.​
6. Sometimes, tell the story to different audiences or under time limits, or answer with a similar story.​

> [!note] The Coffee Shop Test
> If you can read an article, then retell it to a friend in 60 seconds using the article’s phrases, your speaking is already training exactly how you will use it in real life.​

## F. FAQ — FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

> [!question] Q: What if I cannot use all the new words when retelling?
> **A:** Aim to use as many as possible, but do not freeze. Focus on continuity first, especially at lower levels; over time, you will be able to include more new items naturally.​

> [!question] Q: How long should each text be?
> **A:** The exact length matters less than the 5–10 new elements rule. Short texts with 5–10 new items are better than long texts with 30 unknown words that overwhelm you.​

> [!question] Q: How often should I repeat the same story?
> **A:** Typically no more than twice. If you are still unhappy after two attempts, switch to a new text to practice variety instead of chasing perfection.​

> [!question] Q: Is it okay to read notes while retelling?
> **A:** The exercise is most powerful when you speak from memory. You can glance at your highlights if you get stuck, but try to form sentences on your own.​

> [!important] Interview Alert
> Retelling is a natural way to prepare for behavioral questions: read sample answers or stories, then retell them in your own words while adjusting time (10–30–60 seconds) for different interview constraints.​

- Practise:
    - Reading example interview answers and retelling them using your details.
    - Using time constraints to train short and long versions of your stories.
