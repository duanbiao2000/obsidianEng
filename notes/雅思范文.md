## 7分范文

**"I'd like to talk about a game called hide-and-seek, which I played constantly as a child growing up in a residential neighbourhood."**

When I was around seven or eight, my friends and I would gather after school almost every evening to play this game in the courtyard of our apartment complex. The rules were simple — one person counted to twenty with their eyes closed while everyone else scattered and found hiding spots. The seeker then had to track everyone down.

What I genuinely loved about it was the **rush of adrenaline** when you were hiding. There was something incredibly thrilling about crouching behind a bush or squeezing into a narrow gap, barely breathing, hoping the seeker wouldn't spot you. It also required a surprising amount of **strategic thinking** — you had to weigh up whether a spot was well-hidden versus whether you could make a run for the base in time.

Another thing I appreciated was how **socially bonding** the game was. We were a mixed group of kids from different grades, and this game was one of the few activities where age didn't really matter. Everyone had an equal shot at winning.

On the downside, there were moments of genuine **frustration**. If you chose a hiding spot that was _too_ good, you'd sometimes be stuck there for ages, crouched uncomfortably, waiting. I remember one occasion where I hid so well that my friends eventually gave up looking and went home for dinner — leaving me hiding alone behind a bicycle shed, completely unaware the game had ended. That was both funny and slightly humiliating in retrospect.

Overall, though, it's a game I look back on with a great deal of **nostalgia**. It taught me things like patience, spatial awareness, and how to read other people's behaviour — skills I didn't realise I was picking up at the time.

## 7分范文（基于你的真实经历）

**"I'd like to talk about football — a sport that has been a constant part of my life for over two decades, starting when I was around seventeen or eighteen."**

I first got into football in my late teens, which is perhaps a bit later than most people. I remember joining a casual five-a-side group at school, and from that point on, I was completely hooked. What started as a weekend hobby gradually became a serious commitment — I've been playing regularly ever since, and I'm now in my forties.

What I love most about football is the **sense of collective purpose** it gives you. Unlike individual sports, every pass, every run, every defensive tackle is done in service of the team. There's a particular kind of satisfaction you simply can't replicate alone — the moment a well-rehearsed move clicks into place and ends in a goal is genuinely euphoric.

I also value the fact that football has given me **a consistent social circle** across different stages of life. The teammates I play with now are a mix of old friends and newer acquaintances, but the changing room banter and the post-match drinks create a kind of brotherhood that I find increasingly rare as an adult.

That said, playing into your forties comes with **honest challenges**. Recovery takes noticeably longer than it did at twenty. I've had my share of muscle strains and one fairly serious knee injury that kept me off the pitch for three months. There are moments during a match when my mind wants to press forward but my legs have a very different opinion.

But if anything, these physical limitations have made me **appreciate the game more deeply**. I've had to develop better positional awareness and reading of the game to compensate for the pace I've lost. Football has taught me that longevity in anything — sport, work, relationships — requires adaptation.

I genuinely cannot imagine my life without it.

## 🎙️ 7-7.5分范文

I'd like to talk about **Mrs Chen**, my high school English literature teacher. She's honestly one of those people who quietly changed the direction of my life.

**§1 — What she was like** She was a fairly petite woman, probably in her late forties, with sharp eyes that somehow made you feel she could see right through any excuse you tried to make. But despite being quite strict — she had zero tolerance for half-hearted work — she had this ==genuinely warm presence==. She'd always greet you by name, remember what you'd written two months ago, ==and somehow make you feel like your ideas actually mattered.==

**§2 — What she taught** She taught English Literature and Composition. On the surface that sounds dry, but the way she framed it was more like _"learning to think through language."_ She covered everything from Shakespeare to contemporary essays, but the real curriculum was always: _how do you build an argument that holds?_

**§3 — What I enjoyed about her classes** What I genuinely looked forward to was that her lessons felt unpredictable in a good way. She'd throw out a quote and just... wait. No hint, no leading question — just silence until someone committed to an interpretation. That uncomfortable pause taught me more about ==intellectual courage== than any textbook. She also never dismissed a "wrong" answer; she'd just say _"interesting — take that one step further."_

**§4 — Why I still remember her** The reason she's stayed with me is that she's the first teacher who treated students as thinkers rather than receivers of information. When I graduated, she handed me a handwritten note on my final essay — two full pages of feedback that were more like a letter. She wrote that I had "a voice worth developing." I still have it. Honestly, that single line probably explains why I'm as comfortable as I am expressing opinions today.

## 🎙️ 8分范文

That's quite a layered question for China specifically, because the ways people signal wealth have shifted dramatically over the past two decades.

Traditionally, the most visible markers were fairly straightforward — **luxury goods**. Brands like Louis Vuitton or Gucci became almost shorthand for a certain income bracket, and there was a period where ==conspicuous consumption== was almost expected, especially among newly wealthy families. A designer handbag or a foreign car wasn't just a purchase — it was a public statement.

But what I find more interesting is how that's evolved. Among younger, more educated urban professionals, **==overt displays of wealth== have actually become somewhat distasteful**. The signaling has gone quieter and more sophisticated — it's less about the logo and more about, say, knowing which architect designed your apartment, or casually mentioning you spent the holiday in a remote destination ==that requires real money but doesn't scream it==.

There's also a distinctly Chinese dimension around **banquets and hospitality**. Treating a large group to an expensive meal, or gifting high-end baijiu or tea, is a deeply cultural way of demonstrating both wealth and generosity simultaneously. It's less individualistic than Western displays — it's almost ==performative generosity==.

And then there's **real estate**, which for most Chinese families remains the ultimate symbol. Owning multiple properties, particularly in tier-one cities like Beijing or Shanghai, signals not just current wealth but intergenerational financial security.

So I'd say China is actually in a fascinating transitional moment — old money is learning restraint, new money is still finding its language, ==and the whole vocabulary of affluence is being renegotiated in real time.==
