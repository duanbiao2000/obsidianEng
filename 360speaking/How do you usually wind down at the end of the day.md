I've come to think of it less as winding down and more as a kind of psychological border crossing — a deliberate ritual that marks the transition between the version of me that operates in the world and the version that exists when no one is watching. The specifics have shifted over time, but the underlying structure has remained surprisingly consistent: there's a physical component, usually a walk or a hot shower, that serves as a bodily signal that the performance is over; then a mental component, typically reading fiction, which forcibly displaces whatever work-related residue is still circulating in my head.

What I've grown quite wary of, and what I think a lot of people get wrong about winding down, is the tendency to confuse *distraction* with *rest*. Scrolling through social media or binge-watching something feels, in the moment, like decompression, but I've noticed that it rarely leaves me feeling actually restored. If anything, there's a peculiar fatigue that comes from consuming content in that passive, indiscriminate way — a kind of mental static that makes it harder, not easier, to let go of the day. Reading, by contrast, demands just enough active engagement to occupy the cognitive channels that would otherwise keep churning through tomorrow's to-do list. It's not relaxation in the lounging-on-a-sofa sense; it's more like redirecting the traffic.

I've also developed a small but, for me, essential practice of writing a few lines at the end of each day — nothing polished, just a rough inventory of what happened and, more importantly, how I felt about it. It sounds trivial, but it functions as a kind of closure mechanism, a way of physically placing the day into a container so that it stops spilling over into the night. Without that, I find that the boundaries between days start to blur, and I'll be lying in bed at midnight still mentally drafting an email I should have written hours ago.
