I was born and raised in Chengdu, a city in southwestern China that I think occupies a somewhat unique position in the national imagination. It's known, of course, for pandas and spicy food — and those associations aren't wrong — but there's a great deal more to it than the tourist brochure version would suggest. It's a place that has managed, at least until recently, to strike this rather unusual balance between rapid modernisation and a deeply entrenched culture of slowness. People there still gather in teahouses for hours on end, and the pace of daily life has a looseness to it that you don't really find in Beijing or Shanghai.

Geographically, it sits in the Sichuan Basin, hemmed in by mountains on all sides, which historically gave it a certain insularity — it was always a place that could feed itself, that didn't need to rely on the outside world in the way that coastal cities did. I think that self-sufficiency has left a mark on the local temperament: there's a kind of unflappable ease about the people there, a sense that whatever happens, things will more or less work out. Some might call it complacency; I'd say it's more of a philosophical orientation towards life.

I left for university when I was eighteen, and I've lived in several cities since, so my relationship with Chengdu has shifted from something taken entirely for granted to something I view with a degree of nostalgia — and, honestly, a little bit of critical distance. I appreciate it far more now than I did when I was growing up there, but I'm also aware that the version I carry in my head is probably somewhat idealised.
