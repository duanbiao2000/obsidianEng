---

I've oscillated between several different systems over the years, and I think that oscillation itself is probably the most honest answer I can give. There was a phase where I was a meticulous planner — colour-coded calendars, time-blocked days, every hour accounted for. It looked magnificent on paper and, I'll concede, it was genuinely effective for short bursts. But what I found, almost invariably, was that the system would become more important than the work it was supposed to serve. I'd spend twenty minutes reshuffling tasks instead of actually doing them, and the whole thing would collapse under its own weight after a couple of weeks.

What I've settled on now is something considerably less elegant but, paradoxically, more sustainable. I maintain what I'd call a *shallow structure*: a short list of three priorities for the day, written the night before, with no assigned time slots. The logic is simple — if I know what matters most, the ordering tends to take care of itself. I'm also quite deliberate about protecting what I think of as *unstructured time*: blocks where nothing is scheduled and I'm free to follow a thread wherever it leads. Those periods often end up being the most productive, precisely because they're not trying to be.

What I've gradually come to understand is that the real challenge isn't organising time — time organises itself, whether you like it or not. The challenge is organising *attention*. I can have a perfectly arranged schedule, but if I'm spending half of each task switching between tabs, checking messages, and fragmenting my focus, the schedule is essentially decorative. So these days I think far less about when to do things and far more about how to be fully present with whatever I'm doing. That shift, more than any productivity system, has made the most tangible difference.
