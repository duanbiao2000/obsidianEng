It varies quite a bit depending on the day of the week, but as a rough rule, somewhere around six-thirty on weekdays and considerably later — often past nine — at weekends. I'm not one of those people who claims to be a natural early riser; left to my own devices, I'd probably drift towards an eight o'clock start, which seems to be what my body considers its neutral setting. The six-thirty wake-up is, in that sense, a negotiated compromise between my biology and my schedule rather than any reflection of personal discipline.

What I've found interesting, though, is how much the *manner* of waking up matters, independently of the time itself. For a long while, I was a serial snoozer — that ghastly cycle of hitting the alarm every nine minutes, each interval somehow more unpleasant than the last, until I eventually dragged myself out of bed in a state of groggy resentment. At some point last year, I switched to a single-alarm policy, and the difference was remarkable. Not because I suddenly leapt out of bed filled with vigour — that's a fantasy — but because the decision had already been made. There was no negotiation, no agonising over five more minutes. The alarm sounded, and that was it.

I've come to think of it as a microcosm of a larger principle: that the quality of your morning has less to do with when it begins and more to do with whether you approached it deliberately or let it happen to you. A six-thirty start with intention beats an eight o'clock start that you sleepwalk through.
