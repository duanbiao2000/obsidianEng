I think the question is slightly misframed, though I understand why people ask it. The implication behind "is it important to have hobbies" is that hobbies are optional extras — decorative additions to a life whose core structure is built around work, relationships, and obligation. I'd argue the reverse: that having pursuits outside of those instrumental demands isn't just important, it's what prevents a life from becoming purely functional. Without them, you risk spending your days efficiently executing tasks that serve external purposes and nothing else — and that, over time, erodes something fundamental about who you are.

The evidence for this is everywhere if you look for it. The people I know who are most resistant to burnout aren't necessarily those who rest the most; they're the ones who have something in their lives that engages them on a level that has nothing to do with their professional identity. A colleague who does pottery, a friend who volunteers at an animal shelter — these aren't escapes from reality so much as connections to a different part of reality. They remind you that you're not defined by what you produce, which is a correction that most of us need on a fairly regular basis.

That said, I do think the word "important" carries a risk of its own, which is that it turns hobbies into yet another item on a wellness checklist — something you *should* have, another source of anxiety for people who feel they don't have the time or energy. The irony would be quite grim: turning leisure into labour. So yes, I think it matters enormously, but the moment it becomes a duty rather than a genuine pull, it's lost the very quality that made it valuable in the first place.

---
