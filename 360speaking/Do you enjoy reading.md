Here's a C2-level response in a mini essay style:

---

I do, intensely, though I've noticed that the nature of that enjoyment has shifted quite significantly over the years. When I was younger, reading was primarily voracious — I consumed books the way one might consume a meal, for sustenance and pleasure and not much else. I didn't discriminate much; I read widely and indiscriminately across genres, and the measure of a good book was simply whether it kept me turning pages. These days, I'm considerably slower and considerably more selective, and I think what I actually enjoy is no longer the narrative itself but the act of paying sustained attention to something that refuses to be rushed. In a sense, reading has become less about what I get from books and more about what the practice of reading gets from me — which is a kind of stillness that I can't seem to find anywhere else.

As for what I gravitate towards, there's a loose but persistent constellation of interests. Literary fiction is the foundation — writers like Ishiguro or Sebald, who use narrative not as a vehicle for plot but as a way of probing the edges of memory and loss. Alongside that, I read a fair amount of narrative non-fiction, particularly when it takes subjects that seem dry on the surface — urban planning, the history of infrastructure, the economics of obscure commodities — and reveals the human stories embedded within them. I also have a weakness for certain kinds of essayists: people who can take an apparently trivial observation and, through sheer force of intellect and prose, unfold it into something universal.

What connects these, I think, is an impatience with books that give you exactly what you expect. I'm drawn to writing that creates a slight sense of disorientation — that makes you uneasy, or uncertain, or aware that you're reading something that doesn't quite fit the category you assigned it to. Comfort reading, I've realised, is precisely what I don't want from books. I want them to complicate my thinking, not confirm it.

---

### 为什么这个回答达到 C2 水平

- **词汇精准度高**：*voracious, indiscriminately, discriminate, sustained attention, refuses to be rushed, constellation of interests, probing the edges, obscure commodities, sheer force of intellect and prose, impatience, disorientation, uneasy, confirm*
- **句式多样性**：破折号多层补充说明、插入语（*though I've noticed*）、定语从句嵌套、隐喻结构（*consumed books the way one might consume a meal*）、分号连接并列分句、对比结构
- **逻辑层次清晰**：第一段从阅读体验的历时性变化切入，第二段具体描述偏好的类型并举例，第三段提炼出一条底层选择标准 — 体验演变 → 具体偏好 → 抽象原则，三段递进
- **思维深度突出**：没有说"I like novels and non-fiction because they're interesting"，而是将阅读从"获取内容"重新定义为"一种注意力的修行"（*what the practice of reading gets from me*）。对书单的描述不是简单罗列类型，而是精确到**作者风格**（*not as a vehicle for plot but as a way of probing the edges of memory*），再到**题材特征**（*takes subjects that seem dry and reveals the human stories embedded within*），最后提炼为 *disorientation over comfort* 的审美原则。这种从现象到本质的逐层抽象是 C2 思维的核心标志
