I tend to be slightly wary of the word "hobby" itself, if I'm honest, because it carries a connotation of something leisurely and compartmentalised — a pleasant little activity you slot into the margins of your life. The things that actually sustain me don't really fit that frame. I read extensively, but I wouldn't call it a hobby; it's closer to a basic metabolic requirement. I play the guitar, badly and without any ambition to improve, but that feels more like a private conversation with myself than a pastime. And I cook, though not with the enthusiast's eagerness for novelty — I tend to return to the same dozen or so dishes and refine them incrementally over months, which probably says something about my temperament that I'd rather not examine too closely.

If there's a thread connecting these, it's that none of them produce anything of external value. There's no audience, no deliverable, no metric by which to judge success or failure. And I think that's precisely why I gravitate towards them. My professional life is saturated with evaluation — grades, feedback, rankings, comparisons — and these activities function as a kind of sanctuary from that. When I'm reading a novel at midnight or improvising badly on the guitar, there's no one to perform for and nothing to prove. The value resides entirely in the doing, which sounds almost banal when I say it aloud, but it's a quality I've come to depend on more than I used to.

I suppose what I'm describing isn't really a collection of hobbies so much as a deliberate strategy for maintaining some interior space that remains uncolonised by the logic of productivity. Whether that makes them hobbies or something else entirely, I'm not sure — but I know I'd be considerably less functional without them.
