If I had to single out one thing, it would be the texture of everyday life there — the way the city somehow manages to be both energetic and unhurried at the same time. Chengdu has this remarkable capacity to absorb ambition without letting it become the organising principle of everything. You can walk through a commercial district buzzing with new startups and venture capital offices, turn a corner, and find yourself in a narrow alley where an elderly couple are running a noodle stall they've operated for thirty years. That coexistence isn't accidental — it's deeply woven into the character of the place.

On a more personal level, what I genuinely miss is the food culture, and I don't mean that in the superficial, listicle sense. It's not just that the cuisine is exceptional — though it objectively is — it's that eating there functions as a social infrastructure in a way I haven't encountered elsewhere. A meal isn't merely sustenance; it's the default setting for human connection. People resolve conflicts over hotpot, broker deals in unassuming teahouses, and sustain friendships primarily through the act of sharing food. There's an implicit generosity embedded in that culture that I find quite moving, especially now that I live somewhere where meals are often something to be got through rather than lingered over.

I suppose, ultimately, what I like most is that Chengdu doesn't constantly demand that you justify your existence through productivity. It gives you permission to simply *be* — and in today's climate, that feels like an increasingly rare and precious thing.
