That's a question I find myself answering differently depending on the season, to be honest. During the winter months, I tend to gravitate towards more introverted pursuits — I'm an avid reader, particularly of literary fiction and narrative non-fiction, and I also spend quite a bit of time experimenting with cooking. There's something deeply satisfying about following a complex recipe with the kind of methodical patience that, say, a work deadline wouldn't allow. It's almost meditative, in a way.

When the weather warms up, though, my habits shift quite dramatically. I'm a fairly keen trail runner, and I try to get out into the hills at least once a week. It's not really about fitness, though that's a welcome byproduct — it's more about the headspace it gives me. There's a particular quality of solitude you get on a mountain trail at six in the morning that I've never been able to replicate anywhere else. I usually pair that with photography, though I'd hesitate to call myself anything beyond a enthusiastic amateur in that department.

If I had to identify a single thread running through all of this, I suppose it would be a desire for activities that are, for lack of a better term, *unoptimised*. So much of my working life revolves around efficiency, deliverables, and measurable outcomes that I actively seek out pastimes where the value lies purely in the doing — not in what gets produced at the end.
