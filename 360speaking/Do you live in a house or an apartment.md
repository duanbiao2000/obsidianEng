At the moment, I live in a modestly sized apartment on the outskirts of the city centre — close enough to feel connected to the urban pulse, but far enough out that the streets quieten down after midnight, which I've come to value more than I ever expected I would. It's a fairly standard two-bedroom layout: nothing architecturally remarkable, but it has good natural light for most of the day, and that, I've found, makes an extraordinary difference to one's sense of wellbeing — far more than square footage or fancy finishes ever could.

I've actually lived in both houses and apartments over the years, and I think each form shapes your daily life in quite subtle ways. A house gives you a degree of autonomy — your own walls, your own patch of ground — but it also comes with a set of obligations that I found quietly draining: the maintenance, the repairs, the relentless administration of simply keeping a building standing. An apartment, by contrast, is inherently more provisional, more shared. You're part of a vertical community whether you like it or not, and there's a certain humility in that, a reminder that you're not as self-sufficient as you might like to think.

If I'm being completely honest, I don't feel a particularly strong attachment to the physical structure itself. What matters to me is what the space allows me to do — read without interruption, cook a proper meal, wake up to silence. The apartment happens to facilitate those things at this point in my life, and that's enough.

---
