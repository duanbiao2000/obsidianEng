The most honest answer, and the one I find hardest to articulate without sounding ungrateful, is the sheer volume of ambiguity involved. I came into my programme expecting that the challenges would be primarily technical — mastering a particular algorithm, getting comfortable with a new programming language, that sort of thing. And those difficulties certainly exist. But what I hadn't anticipated was how much time I'd spend in a state of genuine uncertainty, not about *how* to do something, but about *what* to do in the first place. Choosing a research question, deciding which methodology is most appropriate, figuring out when a piece of work is actually finished rather than merely abandoned — these aren't problems with clean solutions. They require judgment, and judgment is something you can't look up in a textbook.

What makes this particularly taxing is that it runs counter to every instinct that got me here. Throughout school and my undergraduate years, success was largely a function of diligence: if you put in the hours, followed the instructions, and met the criteria, the results were fairly predictable. Research doesn't work like that. You can do everything right and still end up at a dead end. Learning to tolerate that — to not interpret a failed experiment or an unpromising result as a personal indictment — has been, without question, the steepest part of the learning curve.

In a strange way, though, I've come to recognise that this discomfort is the point. The challenge isn't an obstacle to my development; it *is* the development. The day I stop feeling uncertain about my work is probably the day I've stopped taking it seriously.
