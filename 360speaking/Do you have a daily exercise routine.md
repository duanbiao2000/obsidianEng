I do, though I'd describe it as more of a daily *habit* than a routine, and I think that distinction matters more than it might seem. A routine, in my mind, implies something structured and deliberate — a planned session at a set time with a defined sequence of exercises. What I actually do is considerably less regimented: I run most mornings, but the distance, pace, and route vary from day to day depending on how I feel, what the weather's doing, and how much time I have. Some days it's forty-five minutes at a comfortable tempo; others it's barely twenty minutes of what I'd generously call a jog. The only constant is the act of getting out the door, and I've found that treating that single step as non-negotiable removes a huge amount of the friction that derails more ambitious plans.

The shift from a rigid programme to this more flexible approach was, I have to admit, born of repeated failure. I spent years crafting elaborate weekly schedules — Monday intervals, Wednesday tempo, Friday long run — that I'd adhere to for perhaps three weeks before life intervened and the whole edifice came crashing down. Each collapse felt like a personal shortcoming, which made restarting even harder, because now it was coloured by guilt. What eventually stuck was the realisation that consistency of form matters far more than consistency of content. Showing up every day in some capacity, even at a diminished intensity, builds a momentum that a single perfect session followed by six days of inactivity simply cannot.

If there's a broader principle in this, it's that I've stopped thinking of exercise as something I *do* and started treating it as something I *am* — a person who moves every day, in the same way that a person brushes their teeth. It's no longer a question of motivation; it's just part of the architecture of my day.
