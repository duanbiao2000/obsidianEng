I'd describe it as a transitional neighbourhood — one of those areas that hasn't quite decided what it wants to be yet. It sits on the seam between an older, residential district with small family-run shops and a newer commercial zone that's sprung up over the last five years or so. The result is a slightly eccentric mix: you can buy freshly made tofu from a stall that's been there for decades, then walk thirty metres and find a sleek artisan coffee bar with a tasting menu. There's a friction in that juxtaposition that I find genuinely interesting — it prevents the area from ever feeling generic or sterilised.

Demographically, it's quite diverse, which I appreciate more than I can probably articulate. There are retirees who've lived in the same buildings since the nineties, young professionals like myself who were drawn by relatively affordable rents, and a small but growing contingent of artists who've opened studios in converted ground-floor spaces. What's striking is that these groups don't really interact in any formal sense — they're not organising community events or sharing a WhatsApp group — yet there's an unspoken tolerance that holds the place together. You see it in small gestures: the elderly man who nods at the barista every morning without ever ordering a coffee, the way nobody complains when the studio downstairs hosts a slightly noisy exhibition opening.

If I had to characterise it in a single word, I'd say it's *alive* — not in a vibrant, curated sense, but in the way that any ecosystem is alive when competing elements are allowed to coexist without being over-managed. I think that's increasingly rare in modern cities, where neighbourhoods tend to be either thoroughly gentrified or left to decline, with very little in between.
